# PROJECT_CHARTER.md - Project Charter

This file is drafted by the PI Liaison after the startup interview and reviewed by the human PI before the working group treats it as approved project direction.

## Project title

Urban wildlife corridor geometry and multi-species dispersal

## Environmental system

Urban habitat fragments connected by wildlife corridors, represented as a spatial metacommunity of multiple species with different habitat preferences and dispersal kernels. The project now also treats cities and ecosystems as co-located transport/metabolic networks that must fit into the same geographic footprint.

## Core scientific question

Do hierarchical ecological transport networks support multi-species flow, persistence, recovery, and dispersal more effectively than corridor networks designed mainly around shortest-distance connections between habitat fragments, once travel time, crossing risk, disturbance, and human transport constraints are included?

## Hypothesis or working intuition

The PI's working hypothesis is that shortest-distance corridor webs can create connectivity that is geometrically efficient locally but poor for community-level dispersal, while hierarchical dendritic or fractal-like networks may better organize movement across scales, maintain metabolic flow, reduce high-flow crossing conflicts with human infrastructure, and support coexistence or recovery.

## Starting data, sites, models, sensors, or literature

Initial work now builds from Ty's prior materials in `documents/source_materials/`: a 2021 design-principles DOCX, a Quarto network population-dynamics model, and an R Markdown greenspace scaling workflow using OpenStreetMap and Canadian city data. No empirical species occurrence, movement, or genetic data have been approved or selected yet. Literature support for urban connectivity, graph/circuit corridor design, metacommunity theory, biological transport networks, urban scaling, and constructal/optimal transport theory remains to be curated.

## Intended output

A manuscript-style draft with real, reproducible simulations; simulation code and output tables; figures; and a clearly marked list of assumptions, limitations, and next tests.

## Audience

Initial audience: internal scientific working group and PI review. Possible later audience: urban ecology, landscape connectivity, conservation planning, and ecological modeling researchers, pending evidence review.

## Useful result

A useful first result is a model that can compare corridor geometries under controlled assumptions and identify whether the dendritic hypothesis survives initial simulation, sensitivity testing, and skeptic review.

## Skeptic review focus

Whether the model bakes in the hypothesized advantage; whether dendritic geometry is being compared fairly against optimized shortest-distance networks; whether the species dynamics are ecologically plausible; whether outputs are robust to dispersal rates, competition, mortality, corridor resistance, and habitat heterogeneity.

## Ethical, sovereignty, policy, community, or data sensitivity issues

No sensitive empirical data are currently in use. Future empirical urban applications may involve communities, land access, governance, and policy-sensitive recommendations; those require human and domain review before public claims.

## Actions requiring human approval

Defaults in HUMAN_REVIEW.md and USER_CONTEXT.md apply. Additional project-specific approvals are required before selecting real cities or species as case studies, making policy recommendations, using restricted biodiversity data, or presenting claims as planning guidance.
